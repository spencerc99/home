## Webstones

All of these are artworks that combine physical and digital craft.

## [WORKSHOP] Field Companions: Worn Memory

In this workshop, we will embed stories inside clothing and create a system for passing down memories through generations.

One of my favorite things to do is buying secondhand items. Clothing, books, furniture, and art all have stories of their previous owners. Sometimes they are very obvious, like a jacket with the owner's name written on the label. Other times they give you intimate details without knowing who the person is, like the annotations in a book. Sometimes they are more subtle, like the small stains and scratches left in used chairs and tables. These things make these items more valuable to me than new things.

I love how these common objects start to become historical artifacts because we are giving meaning to them, with our life. There are so many practices and rituals that allow us to imbue pieces of ourselves into objects.

In this workshop, we'll explore how technology can facilitate a new practice for archiving and sharing our personal histories. Together, we will

1. Choose a garment - either one you brought from home or one from Pubrobe's public wardrobe
2. Scan a pre-programmed NFC chip and use a web interface to document your garment's story
3. Create a personal patch using fabric collage techniques
4. Draw designs on your patch using fabric markers
5. Attach your completed patch to the garment using fabric glue
6. The visible alteration becomes an invitation for others to discover the digital stories embedded within

<img src="https://yoshinarinishio.net/wp-content/uploads/2024/03/Pubrobe_01.jpg" />

This approach builds on <a href="https://yoshinarinishio.net/works/pubrobe/">Pubrobe</a>, a public wardrobe project by Yoshinari Nishio, which welcomes the public to offer their clothes to each other as a common resource.

Each garment in that project carries the story that the previous owner had with it.

We will create a system that supports the accumulation of stories. When someone passes the garment on, the next owner can add their own story before giving it away again. This creates evolving narrative objects. Each garment accumulates a growing archive of stories as it moves through different hands.

In daily life, the visible alteration creates opportunities for intimate exchange. When you meet friends and strangers, the design invites them to discover your story.

## Understanding NFC Technology

NFC stands for Near Field Communication. This technology allows two devices to communicate when they are very close together - within a few centimeters.

When you tap your phone to make a payment (Apple Pay, Google Pay, or other mobile payment systems), you are using NFC.

<div class="w-1/2">![Apple Pay](/assets/apple-pay.jpg)</div>

When you tap your phone or card to pay for subway or train tickets, you are using a form of NFC.

Most modern smartphones have NFC capability built in. This technology has become standard over the past several years.

## Workshop Instructions

Please follow these steps to create your worn memory:

### 1. Choose Your Garment

1. Use the garment you brought from home or

### 2. Scan Your NFC Chip

Each participant will receive a pre-programmed NFC chip. To connect it to your garment:

1. Hold your phone near the NFC chip
2. A notification will appear - tap it to open the web interface
3. You'll see a form where you can record your garment's story

### 3. Using the Digital Interface

The web interface allows you to document your garment's story:

  <img src="/workshops/worn-memory/worn-memory-ui.jpeg" />

In the interface, you can:

- **Share your memories**: Did you have a meaningful day with it when you wore it?
- **Log your wear**: How many times did you wear it? When did you last wear it?
- **View the archive**: See stories from previous owners (if this garment has been passed down before)

When you pass the garment to someone else in the future, they will scan the same NFC chip, see your story in the archive, and add their own story to the collection. Each garment becomes a living record of all the people who have worn it.

### 5. Create Your Design

<div class="w-1/2">![](/workshops/worn-memory/worn-memory-patch.jpeg)</div>

Follow these steps to make your alteration:

**Step 1: Design Your Patch**

- Look through the spare fabrics and select pieces that speak to you
- Cut them into shapes that reflect your story or aesthetic
- Arrange them in a collage pattern - experiment with different combinations

**Step 2: Add Details**

- Use the fabric pens to draw on your fabric pieces
- Add patterns, text, symbols, or decorative elements
- Consider tracing your hand as a way to mark your presence

**Step 3: Assemble Your Patch**

- Use fabric glue to attach your collage pieces together
- Let the glue dry according to the instructions
- Make sure all edges are secure

**Step 4: Attach to Garment**

- Choose where on the garment your patch should go
- Apply fabric glue to the back of your completed patch
- Press firmly onto the garment and let dry
- This visible alteration becomes an invitation for others to discover your story

<div class="w-1/2">![](/workshops/worn-memory/worn-memory-garment.jpeg)</div>

### 6. Consider the Future

As you create, think about:

- What makes this garment meaningful to you?
- What story do you want to preserve about your relationship with it?
- When you pass this garment on, what do you want the next person to know?
- How can your design reflect your personal style while inviting interaction?

## The Future of Your Garment

Worn memory creates objects that grow over time. When you are ready to pass your garment to someone else, they can:

1. Scan the NFC chip to read all the previous stories
2. Use the same web interface to add their own story to the archive
3. Create their own alteration or patch to add to the design
4. Pass it on again when they are ready

Each garment becomes a living archive. It accumulates voices, relationships, and memories as it moves through different hands. The physical patches layer upon each other, just as the digital stories accumulate in the archive.
